Section A · Standing

Standing Sequence

7 Poses · 5 Breaths Each

Root → mobilise → expand laterally → open hips → fold forward. The sequence builds from stillness into dynamic expansion and closes by returning to the earth. Practise both sides before moving to the next asana.

1
Tāḍāsana
Palm Tree Pose · Mountain
↑ Inhale — sweep arms overhead, rise onto toes Retain briefly at the top ↓ Exhale — lower arms, heels down
Setup: Feet hip-width, all four corners of each foot grounded. Engage thighs, lift kneecaps. Lengthen tailbone downward, crown of the head floats up. Fingers interlaced, palms turned to the sky on the inhale. Hold 5 breaths — this is the foundation for every standing pose that follows. Re-establish stillness after the sun salutations.
This is the standing equivalent of Daṇḍāsana. Establish alignment here before the laterals begin.
2
Tiryaka Tāḍāsana
Swaying Palm Tree
↑ Inhale — arms up, interlace fingers, palms to sky ↓ Exhale — side-bend right, hips square ↑ Inhale — return to centre ↓ Exhale — side-bend left
From Tāḍāsana. Feet hip-width, press the outer edges down as you bend — the pelvis must not shift. Keep both arms long; do not collapse the waist on the concave side. The movement is a pure lateral — like a palm tree swaying in a breeze, roots unmoved. 3–5 rounds each side.
Lengthens the lateral trunk, intercostals, and quadratus lumborum. Prepares the side body for Trikoṇāsana.
3
Trikoṇāsana
Triangle Pose
Right side · then Left side
↑ Inhale — step wide, arms to T ↓ Exhale — reach right hand to shin or ankle ↑ Inhale — lengthen top arm to sky Hold 5 breaths ↑ Inhale — rise up with a strong core ↓ Exhale — pivot and switch sides
Foot placement: right foot 90°, left foot 15°. Stack the hips over the heels. The key action is rotation — think two panes of glass, not a forward fold. Revolve the ribcage open to the ceiling. Gaze up to the top hand if comfortable on the neck. Bottom arm reaches down, not forward. Repeat left side.
Common error: students collapse the chest forward. Cue "stack the shoulders" and "twist open to the sky."
4
Utthita Pārśvakoṇāsana
Extended Side Angle
Right side · then Left side
↑ Inhale — bend front knee to 90°, spine long ↓ Exhale — forearm to thigh or hand outside front foot ↑ Inhale — extend top arm overhead by the ear Hold 5 breaths · one long diagonal line
Builds directly on the Trikoṇāsana stance. Front knee tracks over the ankle — no deeper. One long diagonal line from the back heel through the fingertips. Press the outer back foot firmly into the mat. Open the chest towards the ceiling — do not collapse forward. The top arm by the ear intensifies the lateral stretch. Repeat left side.
Strengthens the legs and core while deeply opening the thoracic spine and hip flexors.
5
Vīrabhadrāsana II
Warrior II
Right side · then Left side
↑ Inhale — arms open wide to T, gaze over right fingers ↓ Exhale — sink front knee over ankle Hold 5 breaths · strong, steady dṛṣṭi (gaze)
From a wide stance, pivot to the short edge. Right knee directly over right ankle. Hips open to the long edge — not squared to the front. Both arms reaching actively. Imagine you are being pulled apart at both hands simultaneously. The back arm is as engaged as the front. Steady gaze forward — cultivate the quality of a warrior: fierce but calm. Repeat left side.
The hips in Warrior II are open (facing the side). This distinguishes it from Warrior I. Cue students to feel the difference.
6
Vīrabhadrāsana I
Warrior I
Right side · then Left side
↑ Inhale — pivot hips square to front, sweep arms overhead ↓ Exhale — sink front knee, draw shoulder blades together ↑ Inhale — lift and lengthen through the torso, slight backbend Hold 5 breaths
Back heel down at 45°, pressing firmly. Square the hips to the front — this is the key challenge of this pose. Lift chest, gentle backbend without compressing the lower back. Arms parallel or palms together overhead. The back leg is active — feel the psoas and hip flexor stretching. Repeat left side.
Most physically demanding standing pose in this sequence. Allow extra breath here. Common error: back heel lifting, hips twisting open. Keep returning to the squareness of the hips.
7
Pādāṅguṣṭhāsana
Big Toe Pose
↑ Inhale — feet hip-width, lengthen spine long ↓ Exhale — hinge from hips, hook peace fingers around big toes ↑ Inhale — flat back, lift chest halfway (Ardha Uttānāsana) ↓ Exhale — fold fully, elbows bend wide Hold 5–8 breaths · surrender deeper on each exhale
Lead with the chest, not the head. Soft bend in the knees if hamstrings are tight — never sacrifice a long spine for straight legs. Hook the peace finger (index and middle) firmly around the big toe. On each exhale, soften and release a little deeper. This bilateral forward fold neutralises the spine after the Warriors and closes the standing sequence with quiet.
This is the closing of the standing sequence. Invite students to let gravity do the work — no forcing, no reaching. Simply breathe and release.
↓
Transition: From Pādāṅguṣṭhāsana, bend the knees, release the toes, and slowly roll up vertebra by vertebra — or step back and lower to seated. Come to sit at the centre of the mat for the sitting sequence.
Section B · Sitting

Sitting Sequence

4 Poses

Establish the seat → fold bilaterally → fold unilaterally → counter-pose with a backbend to neutralise the spine. The sitting sequence takes the awareness inward and prepares the body for the cool-down.

1
Daṇḍāsana
Staff Pose
↑ Inhale — sit tall, hands beside hips, flex feet strongly ↓ Exhale — press thighs into mat, draw lower belly in Hold 3–5 breaths · establish the base
Legs extended, feet flexed actively. Hands press into the mat alongside the hips. This is the seated equivalent of Tāḍāsana — the pose is active, not passive. If the lower back rounds, sit on a folded blanket to tilt the pelvis forward. The alignment established here is the template for every seated pose that follows.
Never skip this pose. Students often collapse immediately — use 3 breaths here to reset posture before folding.
2
Paścimottānāsana
Seated Forward Bend
↑ Inhale — arms up, spine long, grow tall ↓ Exhale — hinge from hips, walk hands to feet or shins ↑ Inhale — flat back, lift chest slightly ↓ Exhale — soften and fold deeper Hold 8–10 breaths · deepen on each exhale
Lead with the chest, not the head. Feet flexed throughout. Use a strap around the soles if needed — never sacrifice a flat back for the sake of reaching the feet. On each inhale, create a little length; on each exhale, surrender a little more. Bilateral hamstring release and deep spinal extension.
The longest hold in the sitting sequence — 8 to 10 breaths. Allow students to settle and breathe into the back of the legs.
3
Jānu Śīrṣāsana
Head-to-Knee Pose
Right side first · then Left side
↑ Inhale — bend right knee, sole to inner left thigh, sit tall ↓ Exhale — slight rotation, fold over left leg Hold 5–8 breaths each side ↑ Inhale — rise up slowly, switch sides
Right knee bends, sole of right foot to inner left thigh (not the knee). Slight rotation of the torso towards the extended leg before folding. Extended foot stays flexed. The bent knee may not touch the floor — that is fine, do not force it. Notice the asymmetry in the body: breathe into the tighter side with patience. Repeat on the left.
Unilateral fold — reveals imbalances between left and right. Common tighter side is the left hamstring in most students.
4
Pūrvottānāsana
Upward Plank · East Stretch
↑ Inhale — hands behind hips, fingers forward, lengthen spine ↓ Exhale — press palms, lift hips, point or flex the toes ↑ Inhale — open chest, optional gentle head drop back Hold 3–5 breaths · full body engaged ↓ Exhale — lower hips with control
Counter-pose for all the forward folds of the sitting sequence. Toes pointed or flexed — both are valid. The entire posterior chain engages: glutes, hamstrings, back extensors. If wrists are sensitive, come onto fingertips or fists. Lift the hips as high as comfortable. The front body opens completely — the "east side" of the body.
Essential after three forward folds. Do not skip — this neutralises the spine and restores the natural lumbar curve.
↓
Transition: From Pūrvottānāsana, lower the hips. From sitting, slowly lower down vertebra by vertebra onto the back. Arrive supine — the mat supports you completely.
Section C · Cool Down

Cool Down & Śavāsana

4 Poses · If Time Permits

Release the body supine → decompress the spine → final spinal twist → dissolve into stillness. All four poses are optional depending on time — Śavāsana is the only non-negotiable.

①
Bālāsana
Child's Pose
If time permits — include first ↑ Inhale — in Table, lengthen spine ↓ Exhale — sink hips back to heels, arms extended or by sides Hold 8–10 breaths · passive release
Come to hands and knees (Table Top) before lowering. Knees wide apart for a deeper hip release, or together for more lumbar decompression. Forehead rests on the mat. Arms extended overhead or resting alongside the body — whatever feels most restful. Let gravity do the work entirely. No muscular effort is needed here.
The first inward fold — begins the transition from the active world of standing and sitting poses to the stillness of Śavāsana.
②
Apānāsana
Knees-to-Chest Pose
↑ Inhale — knees slightly away from the chest ↓ Exhale — hug knees in, gentle pressure on the abdomen Rock gently side to side · 8 breaths
Lie on the back. Hug both knees to the chest. Inhale: knees float slightly away. Exhale: draw them back in. Rock gently side to side — this massages the sacrum and the lumbar spine. Apāna is the downward-moving prāṇa; this pose encourages release, grounding, and the natural elimination of accumulated tension.
Simple yet deeply releasing. The rocking motion gently releases the sacroiliac joint and lower back — universally therapeutic.
③
Supta Matsyendrāsana
Supine Spinal Twist
Left side first · then Right side
↑ Inhale — arms open wide to T, lengthen spine ↓ Exhale — draw right knee to chest, cross left Hold 5–8 breaths · gaze to the right ↑ Inhale — return to centre ↓ Exhale — switch: left knee crosses right, gaze left
From Apānāsana, open arms wide into a T. Bring right knee across to the left. Both shoulders stay grounded — this is the key. If the knee does not reach the floor, place a block or folded blanket beneath it. Always twist the left side last — this follows the ascending colon and is considered more digestively beneficial. Hold 5–8 slow breaths each side.
The final active pose before Śavāsana. Neutralises the spine after all forward and backward bends. A profound, effortless release.
④
Śavāsana
Corpse Pose
↑ One final deep inhale — lengthen the entire body ↓ Exhale — let everything go Remain completely still · minimum 5 minutes
Feet hip-width apart, falling open naturally. Arms 30 cm from the sides, palms facing up. Eyes closed. Guide the class: "Scan from crown to toes — soften the jaw, the tongue, the hands, the hips. Nowhere to be, nothing to do. Simply be." Allow a minimum of five minutes — ten is ideal. This pose integrates the entire practice into the nervous system.
The most important pose in the class. Do not rush or shorten it under any circumstances. The physical practice is preparation for this — conscious, effortless rest.
